Problem 1 Student ID System
Lincoln High School in California uses a digital ID system for its 1,500 students. Each student receives a unique 7-digit ID number that maps to their lunch account. The cafeteria scanner reads the ID and automatically charges the student's account. The system processes 300 transactions per lunch period.

Given Data:

• Total students: 1,500
• ID length: 7 digits
• Transactions per lunch: 300
  1. Why is the relationship between student and ID number a function?
  2. If one student could have multiple IDs, would this still be a function?
  3. Can the cafeteria system map an ID back to exactly one student?
Guided Steps:
  1. Consider: Does each student (input) have exactly one ID (output)?
  2. Check: Is the same student always assigned the same ID?

Orders over 10 pounds require special handling at $19.99. The pricing structure remains constant throughout the year. A customer orders three items weighing 1.5, 3.2, and 7.8 pounds.

Shipping Rates:

Weight RangeCost
0-2 lbs$5.99
2-5 lbs$8.99
5-10 lbs$12.99
10+ lbs$19.99
  1. Calculate the shipping cost for each item.
  2. Would the same weight always result in the same shipping cost?
  3. Explain why this shipping system represents a function.
Guided Steps:

  2. Apply the corresponding shipping rate

Problem 3 Uber Pricing Algorithm
Uber's base fare in Manhattan is $2.55 plus $0.35 per minute and $1.75 per mile. During peak hours (7-9 AM and 5-7 PM), surge pricing multiplies the total by 1.5x. A rider travels 4.2 miles in 12 minutes during morning rush hour. The app calculates the fare automatically using these consistent rules.

Pricing Structure:

• Base fare: $2.55
• Per minute: $0.35
• Per mile: $1.75
• Surge multiplier (peak hours): 1.5x
  1. Calculate the fare before surge pricing.
  2. What is the final fare with surge pricing?
  3. Identify the domain and range of this pricing function.
Guided Steps:
  1. Calculate: Base + (minutes × rate) + (miles × rate)
  2. Apply surge multiplier to the subtotal
  3. Domain: possible inputs (time, distance); Range: possible fares
Problem 4 401(k) Retirement Matching
TechCorp matches employee 401(k) contributions: 100% match up to 3% of salary, then 50% match for the next 2% of salary. An employee earning $80,000 annually wants to maximize the company match. The IRS limit for 401(k) contributions is $22,500 per year.

Matching Structure:

• Salary: $80,000
• Full match: First 3% of salary
• Half match: Next 2% of salary
• IRS contribution limit: $22,500
  1. Calculate the maximum company match amount.
  2. What percentage should the employee contribute to maximize the match?
  3. Write the matching function M(x) where x is the contribution percentage.
Guided Steps:
  1. Calculate 3% of salary for full match
  2. Calculate 2% of salary for half match
  3. Add both match amounts
  4. Express as a piecewise function
Problem 5 Starbucks Rewards Program
Starbucks rewards members earn 2 stars per dollar spent. At 25 stars, they get a free customization; at 100 stars, a free handcrafted drink; at 200 stars, a free lunch item. Sarah spends an average of $6.50 per visit and visits 3 times per week. She started the month with 45 stars.

Rewards Structure:

• Stars per dollar: 2
• Weekly visits: 3
• Average spend per visit: $6.50
• Starting stars: 45
  1. How many stars does Sarah earn per week?
  2. When will she reach her first free drink (100 stars)?
  3. Express her total stars as a function of weeks: S(w).
Guided Steps:
  1. Calculate stars per visit: $6.50 × 2
  2. Calculate weekly stars: stars per visit × 3
  3. Set up equation: 45 + (weekly stars × w) = 100
  4. Write function S(w) = initial + (rate × w)
Problem 9 Compound Interest Savings
A high-yield savings account offers 4.5% APY compounded monthly. An investor deposits $10,000 and plans to save for a house down payment. The compound interest formula is A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt), where P is principal, r is annual rate, n is compounding frequency, and t is time in years.

Investment Details:

• Principal: $10,000
• Annual rate: 4.5%
• Compounding: Monthly (n = 12)
A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt)
  1. Calculate the balance after 1, 2, and 3 years.
  2. How long to reach $12,000?
  3. Compare with simple interest earnings over 3 years.
Guided Steps:
  1. For year 1: A = 10,000(1 + 0.045/12)^(12×1)
  2. Calculate (1 + 0.045/12) = 1.00375
  3. Raise to power 12: (1.00375)^12
  4. Multiply by principal
  5. Repeat for years 2 and 3
  6. For part b: Set A = 12,000 and solve for t
Problem 18 Solar Panel Efficiency
Solar panels lose efficiency in extreme temperatures. Peak efficiency (100%) occurs at 77°F. Efficiency drops by 0.4% for each degree above 77°F. A California installation experiences temperatures from 65°F to 105°F throughout the year. Panels produce 400W at peak efficiency.

Technical Specifications:

• Peak temperature: 77°F
• Efficiency loss rate: 0.4% per degree above 77°F
• Temperature range: 65°F - 105°F
• Peak power output: 400W
  1. Write the efficiency function E(T) where T is temperature.
  2. Calculate power output at 95°F.
  3. At what temperature does efficiency drop to 90%?
  4. Find daily energy production if temperature follows T(h) = 77 + 20sin((h-6)π/12).
Guided Steps:
  1. E(T) = 100 - 0.4(T - 77) for T > 77°F
  2. E(T) = 100 for T ≤ 77°F
  3. At 95°F: Calculate efficiency loss
  4. Power = 400W × (E(T)/100)
  5. For part c: Set E(T) = 90 and solve
  6. For part d: Integrate power over 24 hours
Problem 30 Financial Independence Formula
The FIRE (Financial Independence Retire Early) formula uses the 4% rule: you need 25× annual expenses saved. A couple spends $72,000/year. They save $3,000/month in index funds earning 8% annually. Current savings: $150,000. They want to retire when portfolio supports their lifestyle.

Financial Details:

• Annual expenses: $72,000
• FIRE multiplier: 25×
• Monthly savings: $3,000
• Expected return: 8% annually
• Current portfolio: $150,000
  1. Calculate their FIRE target number.
  2. Model portfolio growth: P(t) = ?
  3. When will they reach financial independence?
  4. How does increasing savings by $500/month affect timeline?
Guided Steps:
  1. FIRE target = Annual expenses × 25
  2. Use future value of annuity formula
  3. P(t) = 150,000(1.08)^t + 36,000((1.08)^t - 1)/0.08
  4. Set P(t) = FIRE target and solve for t
  5. Recalculate with $3,500 monthly savings
  6. Compare timelines
